# I Spent 18 Years Replacing In-House IT Teams

Here's what I'd check now that I am one.

I worked at two MSPs. Helped build the first one from scratch. Consulted across healthcare, finance, legal, real estate. Every vertical with something to lose. Then I went in-house.

The view is different from this side.

## The MSP Pitch

The pitch I made for years:

- "We can do this better, cheaper, and with more coverage than your internal team."
- "You get a whole team for the price of one hire."
- "Focus on your business. We'll handle the technology."

→ For break/fix, patching, and monitoring? That pitch is often true.

## What The Pitch Doesn't Cover

- Why that SharePoint site was set up that way in 2019
- Which department owns the shared drive with 7 years of client files
- The conversation with the managing partner about why HR permissions look like that
- The reason the finance folder has the access it has

→ Institutional context. Business knowledge. The "why" behind the permissions. None of that transfers in a managed services engagement.

## Why That Matters For Copilot

Copilot governance requires knowing:

- What data matters
- Who should access it
- Why the permissions are what they are

An MSP engineer rotating through 12 clients a week cannot hold that context. They're not bad at their job. The model doesn't allow it.

→ Copilot governance requires the one thing MSPs cannot scale: institutional knowledge.

## What I'd Check Now

If I were still on the MSP side looking at a client's Copilot deployment:

- Who approved the permissions that Copilot is now amplifying?
- When was the last time anyone reviewed sharing links org-wide?
- Does the MSP contract mention governance, or just deployment?
- Who owns the gap between "Copilot is live" and "Copilot is safe"?

→ I already know the answers. I spent 18 years not asking those questions.

## The Bottom Line

MSPs are excellent at infrastructure. They built my career and I'm grateful for every year.

But Copilot governance isn't infrastructure. It's institutional knowledge applied to data access. And that only lives inside the org.

Either side of an MSP contract, here's the test. Who in your org could actually explain why your permissions look the way they do today?

- You
- The architect who left in 2022
- The 50-page wiki nobody updates
- Or the answer nobody wants to say out loud: nobody on staff anymore

Be honest.
